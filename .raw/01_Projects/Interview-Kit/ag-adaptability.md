# OWNERSHIP

> [!warning] **Soru:** "Tell me about stepping up" / "Tell me about taking ownership"

At Combination AB, our tech lead and product owner were both unavailable during quarter planning. When there's a leadership vacuum, someone has to fill it. Nobody was driving the process — no priorities set, no scope defined, no commitments made. The team was going to stall.

I stepped in for both roles. Gathered requirements from stakeholders, went through the backlog, prioritized based on business impact and technical dependencies, and ran the planning sessions with the team. We set realistic goals and committed to a quarter plan.

The team stayed focused through the quarter. We completed all planned tasks before the quarter ended — which was a first. Not because I'm a better planner, but because having someone take ownership of the process meant work didn't fall through the cracks.

I bring this up under Agile because Agile isn't just about sprints and ceremonies — it's about the team's ability to adapt when the usual structure isn't available.

## Sorulursa

> [!faq]- "How did you prioritize without a PO?"
> I talked to the stakeholders directly. Asked them: "If you could only get 3 things this quarter, what would they be?" That gave me the top priorities. For the rest, I used a simple framework: business impact vs effort. High impact, low effort goes first. I also checked with the team — they knew which technical items were blocking other work.

> [!faq]- "Did the tech lead and PO approve your plan when they came back?"
> Yes. I shared the plan with them before the quarter started and got their sign-off. When they came back, they reviewed progress and were happy with the direction. I made sure to document every decision so they could see the reasoning.

> [!faq]- "What did you learn from this experience?"
> That leadership isn't a title. When something needs to happen and nobody's doing it, you either wait or you step up. I chose to step up. It also showed me that I'm comfortable with the PO side of things — talking to stakeholders, prioritizing, making trade-offs. That's a skill I'd underestimated in myself.

> [!faq]- "What's the principle behind stepping up?"
> It's about ownership mentality — as described in the "Extreme Ownership" book by Jocko Willink. When there's a vacuum, someone needs to fill it. Waiting for the "right person" to come back means the team stalls. I'd rather take imperfect action than no action. The PO and tech lead approved my plan when they returned, which validated the approach. But even if they'd adjusted it, we'd have had a starting point instead of a blank page.

> [!faq]- "How do you handle the authority gap when stepping in?"
> I was explicit about it. I told the team: "I'm not the PO or tech lead, but we need someone to drive this. I'll do my best, and when they're back, we'll adjust." Transparency about your role prevents confusion. I also made sure to document every decision with the reasoning, so the returning lead could understand why we chose what we chose. No surprises.
