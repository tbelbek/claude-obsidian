# DISCIPLINE

> [!warning] **Soru:** "How do you fix bad standups?"

25 people, 30 minutes, nobody listening. That was our daily standup at KocSistem. A standup that doesn't surface blockers is wasting everyone's morning, and ours was doing exactly that. People were giving mini-reports about what they did yesterday, what they'll do today, and nobody cared because most of it wasn't relevant to them.

I broke it up. Each team of 5-6 people did their own standup. Focused on one question: "What's blocking you?" If nothing's blocking you, you don't need to talk. If something is, we figure out who can help and move on. 10 minutes max. The people who wanted a big-picture view of what everyone was doing could check the shared Slack channel where each team posted a one-liner.

I bring this up under Agile because a bad standup doesn't just waste time — it teaches the team that Agile ceremonies are pointless, and that mindset is hard to undo.

## Sorulursa

> [!faq]- "Didn't people miss the cross-team visibility?"
> Some did. That's what the Slack channel was for. But most people realized they didn't actually need to know what every other team was doing every day. They needed to know about blockers that affected them, and those got escalated quickly in the small standups.

> [!faq]- "What's the optimal standup size according to research?"
> Amazon's "two-pizza rule" (Jeff Bezos) says a team should be small enough to feed with two pizzas — roughly 5-8 people. The Scrum Guide recommends team size of 10 or fewer. Research by J. Richard Hackman shows that communication overhead grows quadratically with team size — a 25-person standup has 300 possible communication channels. Breaking into small groups reduces that to about 10 channels each. More signal, less noise.
