# TOYOTA MATERIAL HANDLING — Cross-Team Support & Collaboration

At Toyota, my official role was backend development on T-ONE — the autonomous forklift platform. But I ended up taking on a side role that had much bigger impact than any single feature I built.

I created Python test frameworks — TextTest-based acceptance testing with BDD scenarios and fleet simulation supporting multiple AGV types — that simulated fleet scenarios with hundreds of forklifts, different warehouse layouts, and various load patterns. Once other teams across Toyota Material Handling saw what these could do, they started asking for help. I ended up supporting multiple teams with their testing approach — helping them set up simulation environments, write integration tests, and catch regressions before production. The test framework became a cross-team asset — other teams came to me for help setting up their own simulation environments. It wasn't in my job description, but the need was there and I had the skills. Over time, I became the go-to person for testing strategy across teams, not just within my own team.

The other leadership story here is about external collaboration. We needed to integrate T-ONE with a third-party logistics system. Their tech lead was defensive — burned by previous partners who broke promises and blamed his team. I [[ls-empathy|stopped emailing and went in person]], led with questions instead of demands, and turned a toxic relationship into a genuine partnership. He started suggesting improvements on his own and referred us to another division.

## Key Experiences
- [[ls-empathy|EMPATHY — defensive tech lead, in-person visit, became partner]]

## Sorulursa

> [!faq]- "How did helping other teams work practically?"
> It started informally — someone from another team saw our test setup in a demo and asked how we did it. I spent an afternoon showing them. Word spread. After a few of these, my manager was OK with me spending about 20% of my time helping other teams because the ROI was obvious — teams that had no integration tests before were now catching bugs before staging. I didn't have authority over these teams, but they came to me because I had something they needed.

> [!faq]- "Did the side role take away from your main work?"
> Some, but the net effect was positive. My own team benefited too — the test frameworks I built for others often improved our own. And the cross-team visibility meant I understood how different parts of the system interacted, which helped me write better backend code. My manager saw it as a multiplier, not a distraction.
