# CONFLICT

> [!warning] **Soru:** "Tell me about a conflict with a teammate"

At KocSistem, I had a senior developer who wanted a monthly "deployment freeze" — no releases during the last week of each month. He'd been burned by a Friday outage a few years back and was genuinely worried about it happening again. The team of 25 was split — some agreed with him, others thought it was holding us back.

I didn't argue with him. I asked him to stay after the meeting for 15 minutes. I heard him out — he had real experience and real concerns. Then I proposed an experiment: let's track every deployment failure for 6 weeks and check if a freeze would have actually prevented any of them.

After 6 weeks, the data was clear. Most incidents came from config drift and missing tests — things a freeze wouldn't fix. The problems happened because of what was in the release, not when we released it. I shared the data with him first, not with the whole team, so he wouldn't feel called out.

Together, we built a pre-deployment checklist and set up automated rollback. He became one of the strongest advocates for daily deployments after that. The key was resolving the conflict with data and empathy instead of authority — respecting his experience while letting data drive the decision.

I bring this up under leadership because resolving conflict with data instead of authority builds trust — the team saw that decisions were fair because they were based on evidence, not politics.

## Sorulursa

> [!faq]- "Why didn't you just overrule him?"
> Because he was a respected senior developer. If I'd overruled him, I'd have won the argument but lost his trust and the team's respect. The experiment approach let him save face — the data decided, not me.

> [!faq]- "What if the data had shown the freeze was needed?"
> Then we'd have done the freeze. I genuinely didn't know the answer going in. I proposed the experiment because I believed in data-driven decisions, not because I was sure of the outcome.

> [!faq]- "How did you handle the team being split?"
> I didn't take sides publicly. I said "let's collect data and decide based on that" — which is hard to argue against. Once the data was in, the debate was over because both sides agreed to abide by what the numbers showed.

> [!faq]- "What's the principle behind your approach?"
> It's data-driven decision making, but with empathy. The [[ref-agile-leadership#Data-Driven Decision Making|Crucial Conversations]] book calls this "creating a shared pool of meaning" — both sides need to agree on the facts before they can agree on a solution. The 6-week experiment wasn't about proving him wrong. It was about creating shared data that both of us would accept. If the data had shown the freeze helped, I would've supported it.

> [!faq]- "How do you handle conflicts between two people on your team?"
> Same principle: separate the people from the problem. I talk to each person individually first, understand their perspective, then bring them together with the data or facts. I never take sides publicly. My role is to facilitate a resolution, not to be the judge. The Thomas-Kilmann model describes five conflict styles — I default to "collaborating" (win-win) and only use "competing" (forcing a decision) when there's a deadline and no time for consensus.
