# OWNERSHIP

> [!warning] **Soru:** "Tell me about leading without authority"

Nobody told me to implement CI/CD at KocSistem. Sometimes the most important thing you can do is take ownership of a problem nobody assigned to you. There was no mandate from management, no budget allocated for it. The team was stuck in weekly releases and I could see it was hurting us — slow feedback, big risky deploys, fire-fighting instead of building.

I took ownership of the problem. Measured it with data, proposed a pilot, volunteered our team to go first, and did the unglamorous work — fixing flaky tests, being online early for first deploys, writing documentation nobody asked for. Within 3 weeks the pilot was working. Within 6 months it was org-wide. SLA breaches dropped 60%.

The point: you don't need authority to lead. You need a clear problem, data to back it up, and the willingness to do the work yourself.

This sits in my leadership experience because real leadership isn't waiting for a mandate — it's seeing a problem, taking responsibility, and doing the work yourself to prove the solution.

## Sorulursa

> [!faq]- "How did you get management buy-in?"
> I didn't ask for permission — I asked for a pilot. "Can I try this with one team for two sprints?" is a low-risk ask. If it fails, we go back to how things were. If it works, we have proof. Management said yes because there was nothing to lose.

> [!faq]- "What if the pilot had failed?"
> I would've analyzed why, fixed what I could, and tried again. But I made sure the pilot had the best chance of success — I picked the right team, I was hands-on, and I didn't try to change too much at once. First automated builds, then automated tests, then automated deploys. One step at a time.

> [!faq]- "How does this relate to servant leadership?"
> Servant leadership (Robert Greenleaf's concept) is exactly this — the leader serves the team by removing blockers and creating conditions for success. I didn't tell the team "you must do CI/CD." I said "let me show you how it could work" and did the unglamorous work myself. The result was that the team felt empowered, not mandated. The Netflix culture doc talks about similar ideas — "lead by context, not control."

> [!faq]- "What if nobody had followed your lead?"
> Then I would've had a working CI/CD pipeline for my own team, which is still better than nothing. But in practice, when you show concrete results (daily deploys, fewer bugs, less fire-fighting), people follow because the benefits are obvious. The key is making the results visible — dashboards, demo meetings, before/after comparisons.
