# OUTCOMES

> [!warning] **Soru:** "How do you tell if Agile is working?"

At KocSistem, we had all the Agile ceremonies — standups, retros, planning, reviews. But nothing was improving. We were doing Agile theater — going through the motions without getting the benefits.

My test was simple: if a meeting doesn't produce a decision, it shouldn't exist. After every meeting, I asked "what decision did this meeting produce?" If the answer was "none," we either fixed the meeting or cancelled it. A standup that doesn't surface a blocker is a waste. A retro that doesn't produce an action item is just complaining. A planning session that doesn't result in clear commitments is guessing.

Once the team started thinking in terms of outcomes instead of ceremonies, things improved fast. Meetings got shorter because people focused on decisions. Sprint reviews became useful because stakeholders saw working software and gave real feedback. The Agile process started serving the team instead of the other way around.

This sits in my Agile experience because the difference between doing Agile and being Agile is whether your process produces decisions or just fills calendars.

## Sorulursa

> [!faq]- "Did you cancel any meetings?"
> Yes. We had a weekly "team sync" that was basically a longer standup. Nobody could tell me what decision it produced. We cancelled it and nothing bad happened. If anything, people had more time to actually work.

> [!faq]- "What's the concept behind 'outcomes over output'?"
> It's a core Lean/Agile principle. Josh Seiden's book "Outcomes Over Output" makes the case: measuring how many features you ship (output) tells you nothing about whether users are better off (outcomes). A team can ship 50 features and still not solve the customer's problem. I applied this to internal processes too — a retro that produces 0 action items shipped 0 outcomes, regardless of how long we talked.

> [!faq]- "How do you measure outcomes for internal tools/processes?"
> For CI/CD pipelines: deploy frequency, failure rate, developer time saved. For monitoring: mean time to detect, incidents caught before users report them. For Agile process: sprint predictability, stakeholder satisfaction (qualitative). The key is picking 1-2 metrics per initiative and tracking them, not building a dashboard with 50 metrics that nobody looks at.
