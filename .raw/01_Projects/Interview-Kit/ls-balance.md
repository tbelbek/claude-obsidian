# BALANCE

> [!warning] **Soru:** "How do you balance hands-on vs management?"

At KocSistem as Technology Manager, I had 25 engineers. Finding the right split between coding and managing is one of the hardest parts of being a technical leader, and I felt that tension every day. Half my time was writing code and doing reviews, the other half was meetings, 1:1s, planning, and roadmapping. Some days I'd start a code review in the morning and not finish it until the next day because of back-to-back meetings.

I tried doing both in parallel and it didn't work — context switching between code and people problems killed my productivity in both. So I split my day: mornings for technical work, afternoons for people work. I protected the morning block — no meetings before noon unless it was an incident. This worked because the team knew when I was available for questions and when I was heads-down.

The bigger lesson: the most impactful thing a tech manager can do isn't write code. It's remove the blockers that prevent 25 other people from writing code. A roadblock I clear in 30 minutes might unblock a week of work for someone else.

I bring this up under leadership because the coding-vs-managing balance is the defining challenge of being a technical leader — get it wrong and you either lose touch with the code or lose touch with the team.

## Sorulursa

> [!faq]- "How did you decide what to code yourself vs delegate?"
> I coded the things that required deep context that only I had — like the initial CI/CD pipeline setup and the security scanning integration. Everything else, I delegated and reviewed. If someone on the team could do it in 3 days and I could do it in 1, I'd still let them do it — because they'd learn, and next time they'd do it in 1 day too.

> [!faq]- "How did the team feel about a manager who codes?"
> Most liked it — they felt I understood their problems because I was in the same codebase. A few seniors initially worried I'd micromanage their code. I made it clear: I'm not here to rewrite your work, I'm here to stay sharp enough to make good technical decisions and unblock you when needed.

> [!faq]- "What does the research say about coding managers?"
> Charity Majors (CTO of Honeycomb) writes a lot about this. Her take: engineering managers should stay technical enough to make good decisions, but the moment you're on the critical path for shipping code, you're blocking your team. I agree. I coded to stay sharp and to understand the codebase, not to ship features faster than my team. The [[ref-agile-leadership#Mentoring & 1:1s|Manager's Path]] by Camille Fournier also covers this — the transition from IC to manager means your output is measured by your team's output, not your own code.

> [!faq]- "How much of your time was actually coding?"
> Roughly 30-40% in a good week. Some weeks it was 10% because of incident response or planning cycles. I tracked it loosely — not to optimize, but to make sure I wasn't drifting too far from the code. If I went two weeks without writing or reviewing code, I'd feel disconnected from what the team was dealing with.
