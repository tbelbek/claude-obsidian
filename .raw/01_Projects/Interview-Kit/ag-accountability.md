# ACCOUNTABILITY

> [!warning] **Soru:** "How do you make retros effective?"

At KocSistem, retros were complaint sessions. Retros only work if someone is accountable for following through, and nobody was. People would say "our deploys are slow" or "code reviews take too long," everyone would nod, and nothing would change. Two weeks later, same complaints.

I added one rule: every retro produces exactly 2 action items. Each has an owner — a specific person, not "the team." Each has a deadline — a specific date. Next retro starts by reviewing those items. Did we do them? If not, why not? We don't raise new issues until we've accounted for the old ones.

First few sprints, people thought it was overkill. But when the third retro started and we could say "remember the slow deploy issue? Ahmed fixed it two weeks ago, deploy time is down 40%" — people realized this was different. Problems actually got solved. After that, people started coming to retros with specific, actionable suggestions instead of vague complaints.

This sits in my Agile experience because a retro without follow-through is worse than no retro — it teaches the team that raising problems is pointless.

## Sorulursa

> [!faq]- "What if the action item doesn't get done?"
> We talk about why. Was it too big? Break it down. Was it not actually important? Then don't commit to it next time. Was the person too busy? Then we need to make time for improvements, which is a conversation about sprint capacity. The point isn't to punish — it's to build the habit of following through.

> [!faq]- "What's the research on retrospective effectiveness?"
> Esther Derby and Diana Larsen wrote "Agile Retrospectives: Making Good Teams Great" — the standard reference. Their key insight: a retro without follow-through is worse than no retro, because it teaches the team that raising problems is pointless. The 2-action-items rule I use is a simplification of their "decide what to do" step. They also recommend varying the retro format to prevent staleness — we rotated between "start/stop/continue", "4Ls" (liked, learned, lacked, longed for), and "sailboat" formats.

> [!faq]- "How do you prevent retro fatigue?"
> Rotate formats. If you do the same "what went well / what didn't" every two weeks, people get bored and check out. I used 3-4 different formats and rotated them. Also, the best retro energizer is showing that previous action items actually got done. Nothing kills engagement faster than raising the same issue three sprints in a row and nothing changing.
