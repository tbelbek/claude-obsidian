# AMBITION

> [!warning] **Soru:** "How do you challenge teams to improve?"

At KocSistem, the team was sandbagging sprints. A team that always hits 100% isn't being challenged enough, and that's what I was seeing. They'd commit to 20 story points when they could handle 30, so they'd always "succeed." Velocity was flat and nobody was pushing themselves. Sprint reviews were boring because the work wasn't challenging.

I tracked velocity over several sprints and showed the team the pattern. Then I challenged them: "What would happen if we tried 25 next sprint?" We agreed that missing a sprint goal occasionally is fine — it means you're pushing. Always hitting 100% means you're playing it safe.

Over a few sprints, the team started taking on more. Some sprints they missed the goal and we talked about why. Most sprints they hit it and the work was more interesting. Velocity went up about 30% without anyone working longer hours — just less padding.

I bring this up under Agile because sustainable growth means pushing the team to stretch without burning them out — always hitting 100% means you're not learning.

## Sorulursa

> [!faq]- "Isn't it risky to encourage missing sprint goals?"
> It's riskier to never challenge the team. If the team always delivers 100%, stakeholders assume that's peak capacity and pile on more work. If the team occasionally misses, it creates a healthier conversation about capacity and priorities. The goal is sustainable challenge, not burnout.

> [!faq]- "What does the research say about stretch goals?"
> The Accelerate research by DORA found that high-performing teams have a "growth mindset" — they see failures as learning opportunities, not punishments. Setting stretch goals and occasionally missing them is part of this mindset. Google's OKR system explicitly expects 70% achievement — if you hit 100%, your goals weren't ambitious enough. I applied the same principle to sprint commitments: consistently hitting 100% means you're sandbagging.

> [!faq]- "How do you keep it from becoming pressure to overwork?"
> The stretch is about ambition in scope, not hours. If the team misses a sprint goal, the conversation is "what was harder than we thought?" not "why didn't you work weekends?" Sustainable pace is a core Agile principle. I tracked velocity per person-day, not per calendar day — if someone took a day off, we adjusted expectations. Burnout kills velocity long-term.
