# EMPATHY

> [!warning] **Soru:** "Tell me about working with a difficult person"

At Toyota, we needed to integrate our software with a third-party logistics system. Their tech lead was difficult to work with — slow to respond, defensive about everything, treated every request like we were trying to break his system. Emails went unanswered for days.

I stopped emailing and went there in person. Instead of coming with a list of demands, I came with questions: "Help me understand what went wrong with previous integrations — I don't want to repeat the same mistakes." He opened up. Previous partners had broken promises, pushed changes without notice, and blamed his team when things went wrong. He wasn't difficult — he'd been burned. This was about building trust with someone who had every reason not to trust you.

I proposed a different approach: we start with read-only access, give him advance notice before any changes, and set up a shared daily communication channel. I also made a point of giving his team credit publicly when things worked. Within a few weeks, the whole dynamic changed. He started proactively suggesting improvements and eventually referred us to another division.

This sits in my leadership experience because building trust with someone who has every reason not to trust you is one of the most important soft skills in engineering — it unlocks collaboration that no amount of technical skill can.

## Sorulursa

> [!faq]- "Why in person instead of email?"
> Email is easy to ignore and hard to read tone. In person, you can have a real conversation. He could see I was genuinely interested in understanding his concerns, not just trying to get access to his system.

> [!faq]- "What if he had still been difficult after the in-person visit?"
> Then I would've tried to find someone else on his team to work with, or escalated through project management. But usually, when you show genuine interest in someone's concerns, they open up. Most "difficult" people are just people who've been treated badly.

> [!faq]- "What's the principle behind going in person?"
> It's about reducing communication barriers. Email is high-latency, low-bandwidth communication — you miss tone, context, and body language. In person, you can read the room and adjust. The book Nonviolent Communication by Marshall Rosenberg talks about leading with observation and empathy before requests. "Help me understand what went wrong before" is a classic NVC opening — it shows curiosity, not judgment.

> [!faq]- "How do you build trust with external teams in general?"
> Start small. Give before you ask. In this case, I offered read-only access first — no risk for them. Then I gave notice before changes — transparency. Then I credited their work publicly — respect. Trust builds through consistent small actions, not grand gestures. This applies to any cross-team collaboration, not just external partners.
