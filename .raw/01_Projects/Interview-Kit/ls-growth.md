# GROWTH

> [!warning] **Soru:** "How have you grown in your career?"

At KocSistem, I started as a Senior Developer. I wasn't hired for infrastructure or DevOps — my job was to write backend code. But I kept running into the same problem: no production visibility, slow manual deployments, and no CI/CD.

My career growth came from filling gaps, not from waiting for permission. Instead of waiting for someone else to fix it, I built the monitoring POC myself, then pushed for CI/CD, then took on the DevOps transformation. Each step was me seeing a problem, taking ownership, and delivering a solution. Not because someone asked me to, but because the problems were real and I could see how to fix them.

That pattern — see the gap, fill it, prove the value — is what led to my promotion from Senior Developer to Dev Lead, and then to Technology Manager. I didn't plan the career path. I just kept fixing the next biggest problem.

This sits in my leadership experience because career growth comes from filling gaps nobody asked you to fill — not from following a predefined path.

## Sorulursa

> [!faq]- "What's the most important thing you learned going from dev to manager?"
> That the skills that make you a good developer don't automatically make you a good manager. Writing code is about solving technical problems. Managing is about solving people problems — unblocking someone who's stuck, resolving a disagreement between two engineers, figuring out why a team is slow. I had to learn new skills, and the biggest one was listening more than talking.

> [!faq]- "What's your advice for developers who want to grow into leadership?"
> Ship things that aren't your job. The promotion comes after you've already been doing the work, not before. At every company I've been at, the people who got promoted were the ones who saw a gap and filled it — not the ones who waited for permission. The Staff Engineer's Path by Tanya Reilly talks about this — impact beyond your immediate scope is what separates senior from staff/lead.

> [!faq]- "Do you prefer the IC track or the management track?"
> I like having one foot in each. Pure management without coding makes me feel disconnected. Pure IC without people impact feels too narrow. The tech lead or engineering manager role — where you're technical enough to make architecture decisions but also responsible for team outcomes — is where I'm most effective.
