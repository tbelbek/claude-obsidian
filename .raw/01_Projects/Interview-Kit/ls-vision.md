# VISION

> [!warning] **Soru:** "What's the biggest impact you've had as a leader?"

At KocSistem, I owned the technical roadmap for 25 engineers across DevOps, security, architecture, and Agile maturity. Owning a roadmap means delivering measurable results, not just setting goals -- and in my first year, I hit all the roadmap targets: DevSecOps 4 Key Metrics reached 75% implementation, Agile maturity went from 3.1 to 3.7, release frequency doubled, and we achieved zero penetration test findings across all web applications.

The trick wasn't setting ambitious goals — it was connecting each goal to a pain the team actually felt. "Implement DevSecOps metrics" sounds abstract. "Let's measure how long it takes from commit to production so we can find the bottleneck" is something people care about. Every roadmap item was framed as "this will make your life easier" not "leadership wants this number."

This sits in my leadership experience because owning a roadmap isn't about setting goals — it's about connecting each goal to a pain the team feels and delivering measurable results.

## Sorulursa

> [!faq]- "How did you get zero pen-test findings?"
> I embedded security scanning into the CI/CD pipeline — dependency checks, static analysis, secret detection. Issues got caught at build time, not at quarterly pen-test time. After three quarters of zero findings, the security team stopped treating us as a risk and started pointing other teams to our setup.

> [!faq]- "How did you measure Agile maturity?"
> We used a maturity model with 4 levels across several dimensions: sprint predictability, stakeholder visibility, team autonomy, continuous improvement. I tracked it quarterly with the team — not as a report card but as a conversation: "we're at 3.1 here, what's blocking us from 3.5?" The team owned the improvement, I just tracked it.

> [!faq]- "What framework did you use for the roadmap?"
> I kept it simple. Quarterly OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) — 3-4 objectives per quarter, each with 2-3 measurable key results. The objectives came from business needs and team pain points. Example: "Improve release reliability" → Key Results: "Change failure rate below 15%", "Mean time to restore under 1 hour", "Zero pen-test findings." I tracked progress monthly and adjusted quarterly. Measure What Matters by John Doerr is the standard reference on OKRs, but I used a simplified version — no complex scoring, just "did we hit the number or not."

> [!faq]- "How did you handle competing priorities on the roadmap?"
> I used a simple prioritization: if it's blocking the team daily, it goes first. If it's a strategic improvement, it goes in the next quarter. If it's "nice to have," it goes to the backlog. When stakeholders disagreed, I showed data — "this issue costs us 3 hours per week, that one costs 30 minutes." Data resolves most priority debates.
