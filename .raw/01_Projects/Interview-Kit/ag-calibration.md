# CALIBRATION

> [!warning] **Soru:** "How do you handle estimation?"

At Toyota, story point estimation had become useless. Everything was a 13. Nobody remembered what a 3 or a 5 looked like. When everything's the same size, estimation adds no value — you might as well count stories.

I picked 5 completed stories that the team agreed on — a clear 1, 3, 5, 8, and 13 — and pinned them as reference points. During planning poker, instead of guessing a number, the team compared new stories to these references. "Is this bigger or smaller than the login page refactor?" is a more useful question than "how many points is this?"

The real value wasn't the numbers — estimation is about surfacing hidden risks, not predicting effort. When one person says 3 and another says 8, you stop and ask why. Usually it's because one person knows about a hidden dependency or risk. That 2-minute conversation saves hours of surprise later in the sprint.

This sits in my Agile experience because estimation is only valuable when it surfaces risks — if the team just assigns numbers without discussion, you've lost the whole point.

## Sorulursa

> [!faq]- "Do you think story points are useful?"
> The numbers themselves aren't that important. What's useful is the conversation that happens during estimation. If the whole team agrees quickly, the story is well understood. If there's disagreement, there's hidden complexity that needs to be talked about. Planning poker is a tool for surfacing risk, not measuring effort.

> [!faq]- "What does the research say about estimation in Agile?"
> The #NoEstimates movement (by Vasco Duarte and others) argues that estimation is waste — just count stories and use historical throughput to predict delivery. I see their point for mature teams with consistent story sizes. But for teams where estimation is broken (everything's a 13), the conversation during planning poker is valuable. Mike Cohn's "Agile Estimating and Planning" is the classic reference — his key insight is that relative estimation (comparing to reference stories) is more reliable than absolute estimation (guessing hours). That's exactly what we did at Toyota.

> [!faq]- "Story points vs time estimates — what's your preference?"
> Story points, because they measure complexity, not time. A story that takes me 2 hours but a junior dev 2 days is the same complexity — the same number of unknowns, the same integration points. Time estimates create pressure to work faster. Story points create conversations about complexity. But I'm pragmatic — if a team works better with time estimates, I don't force story points.
