# ALIGNMENT

> [!warning] **Soru:** "How do you handle changing priorities?"

At KocSistem, product owners kept changing priorities mid-sprint. The challenge was protecting sprint scope while keeping stakeholders engaged — not shutting them out. Something was "urgent" every other day. The team would drop what they were doing, switch context, and then get asked at sprint review why the original commitment wasn't met.

I introduced sprint goals — one sentence per sprint that describes what we're trying to deliver. When a new request came in, the question became: "Does this serve the sprint goal?" If yes, we can probably fit it. If no, it goes to the backlog. If it's genuinely urgent — like a production outage — we have a conversation about what we drop in exchange.

I also started running sprint demos with stakeholders. Not screenshots or status reports — live working software every two weeks. When stakeholders could see what we shipped regularly, they stopped micromanaging. They trusted the process because they had visibility. Mid-sprint interruptions dropped noticeably.

This sits in my Agile experience because protecting sprint scope while keeping stakeholders engaged is the core tension of Agile — sprint goals and demos solved both sides for us.

## Sorulursa

> [!faq]- "What if the PO insists something is urgent?"
> Then we talk about trade-offs. "We can do this, but we'll have to drop X. Which is more important?" Making the trade-off explicit usually makes people think more carefully about what's actually urgent vs what just feels urgent.

> [!faq]- "How did stakeholders react to the demos?"
> They loved it. Before demos, they had no idea what the team was doing — just status reports and Jira boards. Seeing working software every two weeks built trust. Some stakeholders started attending demos regularly and giving useful feedback that improved the product.

> [!faq]- "Where does the sprint goal concept come from?"
> Sprint goals are in the Scrum Guide — but most teams skip them. Roman Pichler (product management expert) writes extensively about sprint goals: a good sprint goal is outcome-oriented ("users can check out with Apple Pay"), not output-oriented ("finish 5 tickets"). When you have a clear goal, it's easier to say no to unrelated requests and easier to evaluate the sprint's success.

> [!faq]- "How do you handle sprint reviews that nobody attends?"
> Make them worth attending. I stopped doing slide presentations and started showing live working software. Stakeholders could click through the new feature, ask questions, give feedback. When they saw their feedback actually changing the product, they started coming regularly. The key is: the review should be interesting to non-technical people. If it's boring, that's your fault, not theirs.
