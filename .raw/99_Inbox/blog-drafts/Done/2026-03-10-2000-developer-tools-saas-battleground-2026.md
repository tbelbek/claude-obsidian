## C) Full post

AI coding assistants went from zero to $5 billion in three years. That's not a trend — that's a rupture.

And most enterprise software incumbents are still playing the old game.

---

**The numbers are not subtle**

GitHub Copilot crossed 1.8 million paid users in 2025. Not free tier. Not trials. Paid. Cursor raised a $100M Series B and hit unicorn status without most enterprise buyers having heard of it.

Replit put its developer count at 30 million. Linear reported 5x growth driven almost entirely through word-of-mouth inside engineering teams.

These are category-defining numbers. According to a16z's 2025 developer tools market map, we went from zero to over $5 billion in AI coding assistants in roughly three years. Vercel, Supabase, and Railway all hit $1B+ valuations without a single traditional sales cycle.

---

**What's actually happening**

The classic SaaS playbook was top-down. C-suite relationship, multi-year contract, IT procurement, hope for adoption. Product-led growth flipped that by letting users self-discover.

Developer-led growth is different again. When a developer picks Supabase over a competitor, they're not just choosing a database. They're baking that choice into the codebase, the infrastructure, and every future hire.

Switching costs compound in ways enterprise software can't replicate. Win the developer, and you often win the company — not through a contract, but through the codebase.

---

**The tension nobody mentions**

Developers want the best tool for the job. They'll use Cursor even if the company has a Copilot license. They'll use Linear even if Jira is "the standard." They optimize for personal productivity because that's what they're measured on.

Enterprises want consolidation. Fewer vendors, better security reviews, negotiated pricing. Those two things are in direct opposition right now.

Developers are winning — not because IT gave up, but because AI tooling moves faster than procurement cycles can process. By the time a security review finishes, half the engineering team is three months deep into a workflow that depends on the tool being reviewed.

---

**Why this is infrastructure, not adoption**

Developer tools don't end up in a renewals spreadsheet. They end up in the codebase.

When developers leave, they take the next product they build, the team they hire at their next company, and the recommendations they make in Slack channels that never show up in your pipeline. The SaaS graveyard is full of products that had enterprise relationships but lost the developers.

The companies building here aren't building a customer base. They're building dependency.

---

**What the next three years look like**

CFO rationalization pressure is coming. The dev tool sprawl from 2024-2025 will face budget scrutiny, and that creates an opening for whoever can credibly offer "best-in-class AND enterprise-ready" in the same product.

The companies that figure that out first will define the next generation of enterprise software. Not through traditional sales, not through pure PLG — through something that looks more like infrastructure.

So embedded in how developers work that removing it isn't a procurement decision. It's a rewrite.
