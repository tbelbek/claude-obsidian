## C) Full post

The AI/data M&A market spent $146 billion in 2025 acquiring proprietary datasets and trained models. Not engineering talent. Not UI. Data.

Most startups are collecting that asset every day and treating it like a side effect of running a product. That's the mistake.

---

**What $134B actually means**

Databricks closed a $5 billion funding round at a $134 billion valuation. Snowflake peaked north of $70 billion. Both companies built their entire thesis on the same idea: your data is worth more than you think, and the infrastructure to make it useful is worth a lot.

These are not anomalies. Per AI Data Insider's review, AI and data M&A deals totaled over $146 billion in 2025 alone.

That's a market screaming that data infrastructure is where the value lives.

---

**Why the AI shift changed the math**

Before, your data made your dashboards prettier. Now your data trains your models, personalizes your agents, and determines whether your AI feature is meaningfully better than what a competitor can spin up with a generic API call.

That's the gap. A startup with two years of detailed customer interaction data — what users searched for, what they rejected, what they came back to buy after a week — can build a model that a new entrant simply cannot replicate.

Not because the new entrant is dumb or underfunded. Because they don't have the data. You can't buy history.

---

**What a real moat looks like**

Most startups have lots of data. That's not a moat.

The moat comes from data that is exclusive (no one else can collect exactly the same thing), behavioral (real actions from real users, not survey responses), and used in feedback loops that improve the product over time. Most startups have the first. Few have the second. Almost none have the third.

I've watched early-stage companies get acquired at a discount because their data was uncleaned, unstructured, and siloed in three different systems. The founders assumed the buyer would see the potential. The buyer saw a migration project.

---

**What acquirers actually paid for in 2025**

The M&A market has figured this out. The 2025 wave of AI acquisitions was explicitly about acquiring proprietary datasets and trained models.

The acquirers paid a premium for what can't be reconstructed from scratch. If your data can be rebuilt by anyone with a few months and an API, you don't have a moat.

You have a feature. Features get copied. History doesn't.

---

**What to actually do about it**

Instrument everything now. Most startups under-log — they track conversions and churn because those feed the investor deck, but they miss the micro-behaviors that explain why users convert or churn. Every decision a user makes is a training example you'll never recover if you didn't capture it.

Treat your data schema like an API contract: versioned, documented, broken deliberately only when necessary. Chaotic data that accumulates in unstructured blobs is not an asset. It's technical debt that looks like an asset until someone tries to use it.

Build feedback loops into the product. Netflix doesn't have a better recommendation engine because it has more raw data. It has better feedback loops — each watch, skip, and pause makes the next recommendation more accurate, which creates more engagement, which generates more signal.

That loop is what $134 billion valuations are built on.

---

**Two types of founders**

The founders who get this are hiring data engineers earlier, choosing databases with better querying capabilities, and thinking about data retention as a strategic question rather than a legal one.

Everyone else is shipping features and wondering why nothing feels defensible.

In the AI era, every startup is a data company. The question is whether you're building your moat intentionally or giving it away by accident.
