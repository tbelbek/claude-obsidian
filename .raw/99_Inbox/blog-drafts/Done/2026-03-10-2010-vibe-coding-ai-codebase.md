## C) Full post

I wrote about 40 lines of actual code last week. The rest — a full backend service, three API endpoints, and a config migration script — came from an AI.

I described what I wanted, reviewed what it gave me, pushed back once when it went sideways, and shipped.

This is vibe coding. I'm not embarrassed about it.

---

**It's already the baseline**

Andrej Karpathy coined the term in February 2025. His framing was deliberately casual: "you fully give in to the vibes, embrace exponentials, and forget that the code even exists." He was using Cursor Composer with Claude Sonnet, talking to it via voice. The code was almost irrelevant to him. The outcome was what mattered.

A year later, the numbers caught up with the framing.

Per NetCorp's 2025 AI coding survey: 82% of developers use AI tools weekly, with 59% running three or more in parallel. Copilot holds 42% of the AI coding assistant market. Cursor commands 18% and dominates individual developer workflows. A DigitalOcean survey found around 85% of developers use at least one AI tool.

This is not a fringe behavior. This is the baseline.

---

**Where the bottleneck actually moved**

What I've noticed after months of working this way: the bottleneck moved. It used to be in my fingers — how fast I could type, whether I had the syntax right, how well I remembered the API docs.

Now it's entirely upstream. The bottleneck is clarity of thought. Can I describe the problem well enough that the AI understands the actual intent, not just the literal words?

That's a harder skill than typing. Most developers haven't realized they're being tested on it yet.

---

**Why some people are struggling**

The developers struggling with AI coding tools aren't struggling because the tools are bad. They're thinking in code when they should be thinking in outcomes.

They write prompts the way they'd write function signatures — precise where they should be loose, vague where they should be specific. The AI gets confused, they get frustrated, and they conclude "AI can't do my job."

Wrong conclusion. Right symptom.

---

**What Karpathy updated**

By early 2026, Karpathy said vibe coding felt dated — the tools had outpaced the casual framing. He started talking about agents, about coding workflows where the human is less a typist and more a reviewer-in-chief.

His core 2025 insight still held: technical expertise is a multiplier. The person who understands what they're building and why will always get more out of these tools than someone just poking at them.

A joint Microsoft and Accenture study found an average 26% productivity gain from AI coding tools. That's an average. Experienced developers are almost certainly seeing higher gains. Juniors who leaned on boilerplate are probably seeing lower ones.

The tool amplifies what you already bring.

---

**Three reactions, one right answer**

I've watched three reactions to vibe coding in my network. One group resists on principle — they feel accepting AI output without reading every line is intellectually dishonest. One group embraces it uncritically and ships code they don't understand.

A third group, smaller, uses it like a power tool: deliberately, with a clear sense of when to trust the output and when to read it line by line.

The third group is building faster and sleeping better.

---

**What actually changed**

Vibe coding doesn't replace programming knowledge. It replaces the data-entry layer of programming — the boilerplate, the syntax lookups, the loops you've written a hundred times.

What's left is the part that required judgment anyway.

Someone with no prior coding experience can now ship a working prototype. But "working prototype" and "production software" are still very different things, and the gap between them is where experience lives.

Programming as a data-entry job is ending. I'm fine with that.
