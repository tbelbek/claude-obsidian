# OpenAI Killed Sora Three Months After Signing a Deal With Disney. Let That Sink In.

> **Tags:** #blog-draft #medium #ai #openai #sora
> **Date:** 2026-03-25
> **Status:** draft

## MAIN ARTICLE

In December, OpenAI and Disney announced a licensing deal. Sora users would generate videos featuring Mickey Mouse, Cinderella, Yoda. Hollywood executives genuinely panicked. Tech press called it a watershed moment. Writers asked each other, quietly, whether their jobs were gone.

Three months later, OpenAI killed Sora.

Not restructured. Not pivoted. Shut down. Consumer app, gone. Professional service, gone. The Disney deal — three years, by the way — is now a historical footnote from last quarter.

I've been in tech long enough to know that companies kill products all the time. Google does it for sport. But this one feels different. This one exposes something the AI industry has been carefully avoiding.

**The demo was real. The business wasn't.**

Sora's 2024 launch was genuinely impressive. The videos looked like they cost money to make. There was real craft in the outputs. The model understood light, movement, narrative continuity in ways earlier systems just couldn't.

But generating video is brutally expensive. Not "startup runway" expensive — *seriously expensive*. A single Sora video costs orders of magnitude more compute than a ChatGPT conversation. To run it at scale, profitably, you'd need pricing that most people won't pay, or volume that wasn't materializing fast enough.

OpenAI is burning through cash. They know it. Their investors know it. And with an IPO possibly on the table this year, every product line is now getting the same question: does this make money, or does it make press releases?

Sora made press releases.

**The robot thing is not a consolation prize**

Here's where this gets genuinely interesting, and where most of the coverage misses the point.

OpenAI isn't throwing away the technology. They're redirecting video generation into their robotics research pipeline.

This isn't a face-saving spin. It's actually the smarter bet. Robots are hard because they need to understand the physical world at a fine-grained level — not just what things look like, but how they move, how they interact, what happens when you push something off a table. Synthetic video is one of the most efficient ways to build that understanding. You can generate millions of hours of physics-accurate footage and train on it. Real-world data collection is slow and expensive. Synthetic video at scale is neither.

So Sora is becoming infrastructure for physical AI. Less glamorous than generating Yoda on demand. Potentially far more valuable.

**What Hollywood actually learned from this**

The narrative for the last two years has been: AI is coming for creative workers. Sora was Exhibit A.

That narrative was always running ahead of reality. Yes, the capability is real. Yes, it will disrupt parts of the industry. But "AI can generate impressive video" and "AI will replace production studios" are different claims by a significant distance.

Disney got three months of a licensing deal before their partner killed the product. That's not an AI takeover. That's a vendor relationship that went sideways in one quarter. The creative workers who were most scared about Sora can breathe slightly easier today — not because the threat is gone, but because the companies building these tools are still figuring out whether the business works.

Runway is still building. Pika is still building. The category isn't dead. OpenAI just decided it wasn't their fight to win right now.

**The IPO is doing the editing**

There's a version of this story where OpenAI killing Sora is purely strategic genius: redirect a costly consumer product toward high-value internal research, clean up the balance sheet before going public, focus on the things that actually generate revenue.

And there's a version where it's a retreat: the company couldn't figure out how to monetize its most visually compelling product, took the loss, called it a pivot.

Both things can be true simultaneously. Good companies often make retreats look strategic. The question isn't whether killing Sora was smart — it probably was. The question is what it tells you about how hard the AI business actually is when the hype runs out.

Building AI is expensive. Running AI is expensive. The gap between what the technology *can* do and what customers will *pay* for is real, wide, and killing more products than just Sora.

OpenAI will go public. The S-1 will be instructive. When you see which products made the cut and which ones are missing, you'll have a cleaner picture of what the AI business actually looks like without the demos.

My guess: it looks like a lot of hard tradeoffs, made fast, with imperfect information.

Which is to say: it looks like every other business that ever existed.

## LINKEDIN VERSION

OpenAI shut down Sora yesterday. Three months after a multi-year licensing deal with Disney.

Let me be direct about what this actually means.

Sora was technically impressive. The videos it generated were genuinely stunning. But impressive technology and profitable technology are different things — and the gap between them is where most AI companies are currently living.

Video generation is expensive. Significantly more expensive per output than text generation. To run it at scale, profitably, you'd need either pricing people won't pay, or volume that wasn't materializing fast enough.

OpenAI is eyeing a potential IPO. That changes the calculus on every product line. Sora, for all its impressiveness, wasn't passing the test.

What's interesting is where the technology is going instead. OpenAI says they'll use video generation internally to train robots. Synthetic video is one of the most effective ways to teach physical AI systems how the world works — far cheaper than real-world data collection, far more scalable. That's not a consolation story. That's a legitimate strategic bet.

The broader lesson for anyone building in AI right now: the demo is not the product, and the product is not the business. All three have to work. Most teams have figured out the demo. Fewer have figured out the product. Almost no one has cracked the business at scale yet.

The companies that matter in five years will be the ones that worked backwards from "what will people pay for" before they ran out of runway.

OpenAI is trying to get there. Killing Sora might be part of how they do it.
