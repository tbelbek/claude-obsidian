## C) Full Post

The global SaaS market hit $300 billion in 2025. Forecast: $430 billion by end of 2026.

Those numbers should terrify every SaaS founder. Because Deloitte found that up to 50% of Fortune 500 companies are redirecting digital transformation budgets toward AI automation this year. That money was going to SaaS subscriptions.

The math is brutal.

---

**Why seats don't make sense anymore**

The per-seat model was built on a simple assumption: software has value, users need access, users pay per access. It held for decades.

It breaks down the moment one AI agent can do the work of ten users. If you're paying $150 per seat per month for a CRM and an AI agent handles lead qualification, follow-up sequences, and pipeline hygiene for a fraction of that cost — what are those ten seats actually buying?

The answer, increasingly, is habit and switching cost. Neither holds forever.

---

**Stripe already moved**

In March 2026, Stripe launched new billing infrastructure specifically for AI usage-based pricing: metered inference costs, consumption models that track what agents actually do rather than how many employees have logins.

This isn't a side project. It's Stripe betting that the per-seat model is going away fast enough that payment infrastructure needs to change ahead of it. When the payments layer reprices before the market does, the disruption isn't theoretical.

---

**It's already happening**

Companies are swapping Salesforce seats for AI agents handling outbound and pipeline management. Zendesk is facing real competition from LLM-based support systems that cost a fraction per ticket and don't need training or vacation coverage.

HR software vendors whose products existed to route information between managers and employees are watching that routing happen via chat interfaces that cost almost nothing.

None of these replacements are perfect yet. But "imperfect and 90% cheaper" beats "perfect and expensive" in more situations than the incumbents want to admit.

---

**The counterargument**

SaaS isn't dying — it's repricing. The platforms that survive will become infrastructure that AI agents run on, not competitors to them.

Salesforce is building agent capabilities. Zendesk is building agent capabilities. Every major SaaS vendor has an "AI strategy" now. Some will successfully transition from seat-based subscription businesses to platform businesses that charge for outcomes.

That's a viable path. But the transition is brutal.

Revenue per customer compresses. Seat counts drop. Companies valued as perpetual-growth seat-expansion machines face serious multiple compression as the growth engine stalls.

---

**What survival looks like**

The SaaS companies that make it will stop selling access and start selling outcomes. Salesforce's Einstein agent layer doesn't compete with AI agents — it becomes the environment those agents operate in.

But that requires ceding the per-seat model entirely, rebuilding pricing from scratch, and accepting that the customers who stay will pay differently. Most management teams are not moving fast enough, because the per-seat model still generates cash today.

The era is ending faster than most people are prepared for, and the valuations that made sense last year already don't.
